Filling & Emptying Models

Learn how engineers turn the conservation laws into running code — building from a single rigid tank of ideal gas, one equation at a time, to a reacting, phase-changing, multi-cylinder engine.

Fig. 0.1 — The canonical filling/emptying control volume. A lumped chamber stores mass and energy. Resistive ports (valves, orifices, pipes) set the flow rates from pressure differences; a moving piston exchanges boundary work; the wall exchanges heat. Every model in this course is an elaboration of this one picture.

These notes teach the principles of numerical modelling through one richly developed example: the filling and emptying model. We begin with what a model is and why we integrate conserved quantities, then assemble the governing equations piece by piece. Three working Python programs — supplied alongside these notes — are introduced exactly where the theory motivates them, each adding one layer of physics.

How to use these notes

Read in order. Parts I–II are self-contained and assume only first-year thermodynamics and calculus. Part I
Foundations of numerical modelling

L1What a numerical model is — and what it is not

Before we model a tank, we should be honest about what the word model means. Every engineering model is a deliberate simplification: a set of equations chosen to capture the behaviour we care about and to ignore everything else. A numerical model is one we cannot (or choose not to) solve with pencil and paper, so we hand the equations to a computer that advances them in small steps.

Definition

A numerical model is a finite, computable approximation of a continuous physical system: a set of governing equations together with a numerical method that produces approximate values of the system’s state at a discrete sequence of points (in time, in space, or both).

Two decisions define any model: what you resolve and what you assume uniform. This is the modelling spectrum.

  • Distributed models resolve spatial gradients. The governing equations are partial differential equations (PDEs); the unknowns are fields — pressure, velocity, and temperature at every point. Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) lives here. It is faithful but expensive, and often far more detail than a system-level question requires.
  • Lumped models assume each region is spatially uniform and track only its average state. Gradients within a region are not resolved — they are summarised by transfer laws at the region’s boundaries. The governing equations collapse from PDEs to ordinary differential equations (ODEs) in time alone.

Filling and emptying models are firmly lumped. A cylinder, a tank, a manifold — each becomes a single point in a small state space, and the entire simulation reduces to an initial value problem:

$$ \frac{d\mathbf{y}}{dt} = \mathbf{f}\big(\mathbf{y},\,t\big), \qquad \mathbf{y}(t_0)=\mathbf{y}_0 $$
(1.1)

Here \(\mathbf{y}\) is the state vector — the minimal set of numbers that, together with the geometry and the boundary conditions, fully determines the system at an instant. The function \(\mathbf{f}\) encodes the physics: it says how fast the state is changing right now. Almost all of the intellectual work in this course is building \(\mathbf{f}\) correctly. Advancing (1.1) in time is, by comparison, a solved problem — the subject of Lecture 3.

Three kinds of error — keep them separate

A number that comes out of a simulation can be wrong for three independent reasons, and good engineers diagnose them separately:

  • Model error — the equations themselves are an imperfect description (you assumed uniform temperature; in reality there was a gradient). No amount of computing power removes it.
  • Discretization error — you solved the right equations approximately, because the time step was finite. It shrinks as the step shrinks, in a way set by the method’s order.
  • Round-off error — the computer stores numbers with finite precision. Usually tiny, but in Lecture 8 we will see it sabotage a flow model at exact pressure equality, and we will engineer around it.
Verification vs. validation

Verification asks “am I solving the equations right?” — a mathematical question, answered with conservation checks, grid/step refinement, and analytic limits. Validation asks “am I solving the right equations?” — a physical question, answered only by comparison with experiment. A model can be perfectly verified and completely invalid. We return to both in Lecture 13.

The honest workflow is a loop, not a line: pose the question → choose the smallest model that could answer it → derive the equations → implement and verify → validate against data → and only then add complexity, one mechanism at a time. These notes are organised around that last principle. We start with the smallest defensible model and add exactly one new physical effect per lecture.

L2The lumped control volume

The control volume is the unit cell of every model in this course. It is simply a region of space, with a chosen boundary, across which we account for the flows of mass and energy. The boundary may be fixed (a rigid tank) or moving (a piston face). What matters is that we commit to a boundary and then apply the conservation laws to whatever is inside it.

The lumped (well-mixed) assumption

A control volume is treated as spatially uniform: a single pressure, a single temperature, a single composition describe its entire contents at each instant. This is valid when internal transport (mixing, conduction, acoustic equilibration) is fast compared with the rate at which the boundaries change the state. It is the assumption that turns a field into a point, and a PDE into an ODE.

With that assumption, the contents of the volume are pinned down by remarkably few numbers. We will use the conserved extensive quantities:

$$ m(t)\ \text{— total mass [kg]}, \qquad U(t)\ \text{— total internal energy [J]}, \qquad V(t)\ \text{— volume [m}^3\text{]} $$
(2.1)

From these three, every intensive property follows. Density and specific internal energy are immediate,

$$ \rho = \frac{m}{V}, \qquad u = \frac{U}{m}, $$
(2.2)

and the remaining thermodynamic state — pressure, temperature, enthalpy, entropy, vapour quality — is recovered from an equation of state, which we will write abstractly as a function of the two intensive coordinates \((\rho,u)\):

$$ p = p(\rho,u),\quad T = T(\rho,u),\quad h = h(\rho,u),\quad x = x(\rho,u). $$
(2.3)

For an ideal gas this closure is algebraic and famous: \(p = \rho R T\), \(u = c_v T\), \(h = c_p T\). For steam, refrigerants, or any real fluid it comes from property tables or libraries (Lecture 9). The structure of the model does not change with the fluid — only the function that evaluates (2.3) does. This separation is the single most important architectural idea in the course.

Why states \((m,U,V)\) and not \((p,T)\)?

Mass and energy are conserved: they obey exact balance laws with clean physical source terms (flows in, flows out, heat, work). Pressure and temperature are not conserved — they are consequences. If we integrate \(p\) and \(T\) directly we must invent ad-hoc rate equations for them and we quietly violate conservation at every step. If we integrate \(m\), \(U\) (and \(V\)) and recover \(p,T\) from the equation of state, conservation holds by construction, the same code works for liquid, gas, and two-phase, and the awkward behaviour of \(p\) and \(T\) across a phase boundary never touches the integrator. Every companion model carries the state vector \([m,\,U]\) (with \(V\) prescribed) for exactly this reason.

The generic balance, valid for any conserved quantity \(\Phi\) inside the volume, reads “rate of accumulation = net rate in across the boundary + net rate of generation inside”. The next several lectures are nothing more than writing this statement out, in turn, for mass (no generation), energy (heat, work, and convected enthalpy), and — once we reach chemistry — chemical species (with reaction as the generation term).

L3Advancing the state in time

Granting that we can build \(\mathbf{f}\), how do we march (1.1) forward? A time-integration method estimates \(\mathbf{y}(t+\Delta t)\) from \(\mathbf{y}(t)\) using one or more evaluations of \(\mathbf{f}\). The simplest is the explicit (forward) Euler step,

$$ \mathbf{y}_{n+1} = \mathbf{y}_n + \Delta t\,\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{y}_n,t_n), $$
(3.1)

which simply follows the current slope for one step. It is transparent and we use it in the Lecture 4 sandbox to build intuition. But its error per step scales as \(\Delta t^2\) and it is easily driven unstable, so production models use something better.

The workhorse is the classical fourth-order Runge–Kutta (RK4) method, which samples the slope four times across the step and combines them so that errors through third order cancel:

$$ \begin{aligned} \mathbf{k}_1 &= \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{y}_n,\,t_n), & \mathbf{k}_2 &= \mathbf{f}\!\left(\mathbf{y}_n+\tfrac{\Delta t}{2}\mathbf{k}_1,\ t_n+\tfrac{\Delta t}{2}\right),\\[2pt] \mathbf{k}_3 &= \mathbf{f}\!\left(\mathbf{y}_n+\tfrac{\Delta t}{2}\mathbf{k}_2,\ t_n+\tfrac{\Delta t}{2}\right), & \mathbf{k}_4 &= \mathbf{f}\!\left(\mathbf{y}_n+\Delta t\,\mathbf{k}_3,\ t_n+\Delta t\right), \end{aligned} $$
(3.2)
$$ \mathbf{y}_{n+1} = \mathbf{y}_n + \frac{\Delta t}{6}\big(\mathbf{k}_1 + 2\mathbf{k}_2 + 2\mathbf{k}_3 + \mathbf{k}_4\big). $$
(3.3)

All three companion models use exactly this fixed-step RK4, advancing in crank angle rather than time (the two differ only by the constant engine speed \(\omega\), since \(dt = d\theta/\omega\)). Two ideas govern whether the march succeeds.

Accuracy: local order and global order

The local truncation error is what one step introduces; the global error accumulates over a fixed interval. A method of order \(p\) has global error \(\mathcal{O}(\Delta t^{p})\): Euler is first order, RK4 is fourth. Halving the step cuts Euler’s error in half but RK4’s by sixteen — which is why RK4 reaches engineering accuracy at step sizes where Euler is hopeless. The practical verification test is step refinement: halve \(\Delta t\); if the answer barely moves, discretization error is under control.

Stability and stiffness

Even an accurate method can blow up if the step is too large for the fastest dynamics present. An explicit method is only stable when \(\Delta t\) is smaller than the shortest physical time scale in \(\mathbf{f}\). When a system contains time scales separated by many orders of magnitude — say, millisecond gas exchange alongside microsecond combustion chemistry — it is called stiff, and forcing an explicit method to take microsecond steps for the whole simulation is ruinous.

A preview of the central numerical tensions

Three difficulties will recur, and each gets a deliberate treatment later: (i) the equation of state must be evaluated robustly even on the saturation boundary (Lecture 9); (ii) chemistry is stiff and cannot share the transport step — we split it off (Lecture 11); and (iii) the flow laws contain a square root of a pressure difference that misbehaves at equality — we guarantee a positive argument (Lecture 8). Hold these in mind; the foundations are now in place to build the model itself.

Part II
The filling & emptying model

L4One chamber: the mass and energy balances

We now write \(\mathbf{f}\) for a single control volume connected to one or more reservoirs. Start with mass. With the sign convention that inflow is positive, the rate of change of the contents is the algebraic sum over all ports:

$$ \frac{dm}{dt} = \sum_{k} \dot m_k, \qquad \dot m_k > 0 \ \text{(into CV)},\quad \dot m_k < 0 \ \text{(out of CV)}. $$
(4.1)

For the textbook case of one inlet and one outlet this is just \(\dot m = \dot m_{\text{in}} - \dot m_{\text{out}}\). The single signed sum (4.1) is more general and is what we implement: it handles filling, emptying, and — crucially — backflow, where a port that is nominally an inlet temporarily reverses. We will not need a separate equation for any of these cases.

Energy is the heart of the model. The unsteady first law for an open, possibly moving control volume states that the stored internal energy changes through heat transfer, work, moving-boundary work, and the enthalpy carried by mass crossing the boundary:

$$ \frac{dU}{dt} = \dot Q_{\text{CV}} \;+\; \dot W_{\text{on}} \;-\; p_{\text{CV}}\frac{dV}{dt} \;+\; \sum_{k}\dot H_{k}. $$
(4.2)

Here \(U=mu\) is the internal energy inside the boundary, \(\dot Q_{\text{CV}}\) is heat into the volume, \(\dot W_{\text{on}}\) is any shaft/electrical work done on the fluid, and \(-p\,dV/dt\) is the moving-boundary (piston) work — deferred to Lecture 5, where it does the useful work of an engine. The convective terms \(\dot H_k\) carry energy with flowing mass and deserve care.

Upwinding: the convected enthalpy follows the flow

A flowing stream carries enthalpy (not internal energy) because it does flow work pushing into or out of the volume. The enthalpy it carries is that of the donor — the upstream side. On inflow, the stream arrives with the reservoir’s enthalpy; on outflow, it leaves carrying the control volume’s own enthalpy:

$$ \dot H_{k} = \begin{cases} \dot m_k\, h_{\text{in},k}, & \dot m_k > 0 \quad(\text{donor = reservoir})\\[3pt] \dot m_k\, h_{\text{CV}}, & \dot m_k < 0 \quad(\text{donor = CV}). \end{cases} $$
(4.3)

This single upwinded rule is what makes a port automatically symmetric to flow reversal: the same code computes the right energy term whether the port is filling or emptying, because the choice of donor is re-made each evaluation from the instantaneous pressures. Every companion model implements (4.3) in one line of its derivative routine; we will see it shortly.

Collecting terms, the complete ODE system for a single chamber is

$$ \boxed{\;\frac{dm}{dt} = \sum_k \dot m_k, \qquad \frac{dU}{dt} = \dot Q_{\text{CV}} + \dot W_{\text{on}} - p_{\text{CV}}\frac{dV}{dt} + \sum_k \dot H_k\;} $$
(4.4)

closed at each step by \(\rho=m/V\), \(u=U/m\), and the equation of state (2.3). That is the entire filling/emptying model. Everything that follows — pistons, real fluids, choked valves, combustion, manifolds — is a more elaborate way of evaluating the four terms on the right-hand side of (4.4).

Filling, emptying, and a result worth remembering

Consider the simplest non-trivial case: a rigid (\(dV/dt=0\)), adiabatic (\(\dot Q=0\)) tank with no shaft work, filled from a reservoir. Then (4.4) collapses to \(\dot U = \dot m_{\text{in}}h_{\text{in}}\). Because the incoming stream delivers enthalpy but the tank stores internal energy, the difference \(h-u = p/\rho\) is deposited as extra thermal energy: an initially empty adiabatic tank filled with an ideal gas reaches a final temperature of \(\gamma\,T_{\text{reservoir}}\) — up to 40 % hotter than the gas supplying it. This counter-intuitive heating is a direct, testable consequence of the convected-enthalpy term, and it is the first thing you should verify in any new implementation. In the code · the derivative routine

In Model A the entire system (4.4) is the method SingleCylinderEngine.deriv. Its core is a near-verbatim transcription of the boxed equations — the flash for closure, the two valve calls returning signed flow and upwinded enthalpy, then the mass and energy sums:

single_cylinder_hem.pyexcerpt · deriv
def deriv(self, theta_deg, m, U):
    V  = self.cyl.V(theta_deg)
    st = self.cv_fluid.flash_du(m / V, U / m)        # (rho,u) -> p,T,h,x  [Eq 2.2-2.3]

    Qw   = (self.cyl.h_woschni(st["p"], st["T"])
            * self.cyl.A_wall(theta_deg) * (self.cyl.T_wall - st["T"]))   # Q_CV  [L7]
    dVdt = self.cyl.dVdtheta(theta_deg) * self.cyl.omega                  # dV/dt [L5]

    md_in, h_in = self.intake.mass_flow(theta_deg, st, self.supply.state)      # signed, upwinded
    md_ex, h_ex = self.exhaust.mass_flow(theta_deg, st, self.exhaust_res.state)

    dm = md_in + md_ex                                          # Eq 4.1
    dU = Qw - st["p"] * dVdt + md_in * h_in + md_ex * h_ex      # Eq 4.4
    return dm, dU, info

L5Moving boundaries and work

If the boundary moves, the fluid does work on it. The moving-boundary term \(-p\,dV/dt\) in (4.2) is the bridge between a passive vessel and a working machine: when the volume expands (\(dV/dt>0\)) the fluid loses internal energy doing work on the piston; when it is compressed, it gains. To use this term we need the volume and its rate as functions of the crank position.

For a slider-crank mechanism — the kinematics of essentially every reciprocating engine and compressor — the piston displacement from top dead centre is

$$ s(\theta) = a(1-\cos\theta) + \Big(\ell - \sqrt{\ell^2 - a^2\sin^2\theta}\Big), $$
(5.1)

where \(a=S/2\) is the crank radius (half the stroke \(S\)) and \(\ell\) is the connecting-rod length. The instantaneous volume and its derivative with respect to crank angle then follow from the bore area \(A_p\):

$$ V(\theta) = V_c + A_p\,s(\theta), \qquad \frac{dV}{d\theta} = A_p\,a\sin\theta\left(1 + \frac{a\cos\theta}{\sqrt{\ell^2 - a^2\sin^2\theta}}\right), $$
(5.2)

with \(V_c\) the clearance volume at top dead centre, fixed by the compression ratio \(r_c\) through \(V_c = V_d/(r_c-1)\) and the displacement \(V_d = A_p S\). Because the integrator advances in crank angle, the rate the energy equation needs is \(dV/dt = (dV/d\theta)\,\omega\). The figure shows the geometry; equation (5.2) is exactly what the companion models evaluate.

Fig. 5.1 — Slider-crank kinematics. The crank angle \(\theta\) maps to a volume \(V(\theta)\) through (5.1)–(5.2). The same routine supplies the wall area \(A_{\text{wall}}(\theta)=2A_p+\pi B\,s(\theta)\) used by the heat-transfer model of Lecture 7.

In the code · geometry shared by all three models

The Cylinder class encapsulates (5.1)–(5.2). Model B and Model C import it unchanged — the geometry is orthogonal to the fluid and the network, which is precisely the modular payoff of separating the state equations from their closure.

single_cylinder_hem.pyexcerpt · Cylinder
def _s(self, th):                                   # piston travel from TDC, Eq 5.1
    return self.a + self.l - (self.a * np.cos(th)
                              + np.sqrt(self.l**2 - (self.a * np.sin(th))**2))

def V(self, theta_deg):                             # Eq 5.2 (left)
    return self.Vc + self.Ap * self._s(np.deg2rad(theta_deg))

def dVdtheta(self, theta_deg):                      # Eq 5.2 (right), per radian
    th   = np.deg2rad(theta_deg)
    root = np.sqrt(self.l**2 - (self.a * np.sin(th))**2)
    ds   = self.a * np.sin(th) * (1.0 + self.a * np.cos(th) / root)
    return self.Ap * ds

L6Resistive elements: orifices, valves, and the flow law

The reservoirs and the control volume are connected by resistive elements — valves, orifices, pipes, restrictions. A resistance does not store mass or energy; it converts a pressure difference into a mass flow. Choosing what counts as “resistance” and what counts as “control volume” is the modeller’s decision, and the cleanest choice for lumped models is tank-only control volumes with the resistances sitting on the boundary: their pressure drop sets \(\dot m\), and their heat loss merely modifies the enthalpy delivered across the boundary.

A generic hydraulic/pneumatic resistance relates pressure drop to a dynamic head through a loss coefficient \(K\). With \(v=\dot m/(\rho A)\),

$$ \Delta p_{\text{loss}} = K\,\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{K}{2\rho A^2}\,\dot m\,|\dot m|, \qquad K = f\frac{L}{D} + \sum K_{\text{minor}}, $$
(6.1)

where the product \(\dot m\,|\dot m|\) carries the sign so the same expression describes forward flow and backflow. Inverting for the orifice case — where acceleration through the restriction dominates — gives the form you will recognise from any fluid-mechanics text:

$$ \dot m = C_d A\,\sqrt{2\,\rho_{\text{up}}\,|\Delta p|}\;\operatorname{sgn}(\Delta p), \qquad \Delta p = p_{\text{up}} - p_{\text{down}}. $$
(6.2)

The discharge coefficient \(C_d\) lumps the vena-contracta and viscous losses; \(\rho_{\text{up}}\) is the density on the high-pressure (donor) side. Equation (6.2) is the incompressible orifice; for a gas with a large pressure ratio it must be replaced by a compressible, possibly choked, model (Lecture 10). The structural points, however, are already visible here.

Sign convention and backflow are one mechanism

The donor (upstream) side is re-selected every evaluation from the instantaneous pressures. Whichever reservoir/CV is at higher pressure feeds the restriction; its density and enthalpy are used; the sign of \(\dot m\) records the direction. There is no special “reverse-flow” branch — reversal is just a relabelling of which side is the donor. This is why a steam expander whose exhaust valve opens slightly below header pressure can suck process steam back into the cylinder for part of the stroke and the model handles it without a single extra line.

Real valves do not snap open. A physical lift profile opens and closes smoothly, and a discontinuous area would inject high-frequency transients that wreck a fixed-step integrator. The companion models therefore schedule the effective area with a \(\sin^2\) ramp that is exactly zero at opening and closing and smoothly unity in between, wrapped so a window may cross the cycle boundary:

single_cylinder_hem.pyexcerpt · valve lift & donor
def area(self, theta_deg):
    span = (self.close_deg - self.open_deg) % 360.0
    loc  = (theta_deg - self.open_deg) % 360.0
    if loc >= span:
        return 0.0
    r = min(self.ramp_deg, 0.5 * span)
    if loc < r:                                          # smooth opening ramp
        f = np.sin(0.5 * np.pi * loc / r) ** 2
    elif loc > span - r:                                 # smooth closing ramp
        f = np.sin(0.5 * np.pi * (span - loc) / r) ** 2
    else:
        f = 1.0
    return self.A_max * f

def mass_flow(self, theta_deg, cyl_state, res_state):
    A_eff = self.Cd * self.area(theta_deg)
    if A_eff <= 0.0:
        return 0.0, cyl_state["h"]
    # donor upwinding: the higher-pressure side feeds the nozzle  [Eq 6.2]
    if res_state["p"] >= cyl_state["p"]:
        up, dn, sign = res_state, cyl_state, +1.0
    else:
        up, dn, sign = cyl_state, res_state, -1.0
    ...
    h_tilde = h_delivered if sign > 0 else cyl_state["h"]   # upwinded enthalpy [Eq 4.3]
    return sign * mdot, h_tilde

L7Heat transfer — in the volume and in the resistance

Two distinct heat-transfer interactions appear, and conflating them is a common error. The first is between the control-volume contents and its walls; the second is heat lost by fluid while it passes through a resistance.

Wall heat transfer. The convective exchange with the wall is the Newton-cooling form

$$ \dot Q_{\text{CV}} = h_c\,A_{\text{wall}}(\theta)\,\big(T_{\text{wall}} - T_{\text{CV}}\big), $$
(7.1)

with the wetted area itself a function of crank angle, \(A_{\text{wall}}(\theta)=2A_p+\pi B\,s(\theta)\). For in-cylinder gases the convective coefficient \(h_c\) is supplied by an empirical correlation. The companion models use the Woschni correlation, in which the characteristic gas velocity scales with mean piston speed \(c_m\):

$$ h_c = 3.26\,B^{-0.2}\,p^{\,0.8}\,T^{-0.55}\,w^{\,0.8}, \qquad w = 2.28\,c_m \;\;[\text{p in kPa, SI elsewhere}]. $$
(7.2)

Correlations like (7.2) are model error made explicit — they are calibrated fits, not first principles, and swapping correlations is a legitimate way to bound the uncertainty they introduce.

Resistance heat loss. A pipe or valve can cool (or heat) the fluid before it reaches the chamber. Within the resistance, neglecting storage and shaft work, the steady-flow energy balance says the stream’s enthalpy changes by the heat added per unit mass:

$$ h_{0,\text{delivered}} = h_{\text{up}} - \frac{\dot Q_{\text{loss},R}}{|\dot m|}, \qquad \dot Q_{\text{loss},R} = UA_R\,(T_{\text{up}} - T_{\text{wall},R}). $$
(7.3)
Pitfall · whose energy budget?

An inlet resistance’s heat loss changes the enthalpy entering the control volume — it belongs in \(\dot H_{\text{in}}\). An outlet resistance’s heat loss happens after the mass has left the chosen boundary; it changes the downstream delivered state but does not appear in the control-volume energy equation at all. Getting this attribution wrong silently breaks the energy balance. Note also the \(1/|\dot m|\) in (7.3): as flow vanishes it blows up, so the implementation clips it — foreshadowing Lecture 8.

single_cylinder_hem.pyexcerpt · wall & port heat
def h_woschni(self, p, T):                       # Eq 7.2
    w = 2.28 * self.c_m                          # char. velocity ~ mean piston speed
    return 3.26 * self.B**-0.2 * (p/1000.0)**0.8 * T**-0.55 * w**0.8

# ... inside HEMValve.mass_flow: port heat loss reduces delivered enthalpy [Eq 7.3]
if self.UA_port > 0.0 and mdot > 0.0:
    q = self.UA_port * (up["T"] - self.T_wall_port)        # +ve: fluid hotter than wall
    dh_loss = np.clip(q / max(mdot, 1e-6), -2.0e5, 2.0e5)  # cap to survive tiny mdot
h_delivered = up["h"] - dh_loss

L8Numerical robustness: the always-positive pressure loss

The flow law (6.2) — and its compressible successor — contains \(\sqrt{|\Delta p|}\) and selects a donor by comparing two pressures. Both operations are fragile at the precise moment the two pressures are equal: at start-up, at the instant of flow reversal, or whenever a valve opens onto a matched receiver. A naive implementation hits a square root of a (possibly slightly negative, due to round-off) number, or an inverted search bracket, and produces a NaN that poisons the entire integration.

The user-imposed rule that cures this is simple to state: there is always a strictly positive pressure loss across an orifice. The effective downstream pressure the flow solver sees is forced to lie just below the upstream pressure:

$$ p_{\text{down,eff}} = \min\!\big(\,p_{\text{up}} - \epsilon(p_{\text{up}}),\ p_{\text{down}}\,\big). $$
(8.1)
Pitfall · a fixed tiny constant does not work at engineering pressures

You cannot subtract a fixed small number such as machine epsilon (\(2.22\times10^{-16}\)) from a pressure of order \(10^5\) Pa: floating-point numbers near \(10^5\) are spaced about \(10^{-11}\) Pa apart, so the subtraction is rounded straight back to \(10^5\) and nothing changes. The correct \(\epsilon\) is one unit in the last place (one ULP) at the magnitude of \(p_{\text{up}}\) — the smallest decrement the representation can actually register. In NumPy that is np.nextafter(p_in, -np.inf) (the exact analogue of MATLAB’s p_in - eps(p_in)).

single_cylinder_hem.pyexcerpt · the stabilisation rule
def stabilized_outlet_pressure(p_in, p_out):
    """Largest representable pressure strictly below p_in, capped by the
    actual downstream pressure of the current iteration."""
    return min(np.nextafter(p_in, -np.inf), p_out)

With (8.1) in force, the flow-solver search interval \([\,p_{\text{down,eff}},\,p_{\text{up}}\,]\) can never invert and the argument of every square root is non-negative — even at exact pressure equality. The cost is a vanishingly small, physically harmless flow at matched conditions; the benefit is an integration that never sees a NaN. The same idea recurs as gentle floors on the conserved states (a minimum mass and energy, so \(\rho=m/V\) and \(u=U/m\) stay finite) and as the \(\max(\dot m,\,\dot m_{\min})\) guard already seen in the port-heat term (7.3).

The robustness mindset

Physical models contain operations that are exact in real arithmetic but singular in floating point at measure-zero events — equality, emptiness, zero flow. A robust implementation identifies each such event in advance and replaces the singular operation with one that is continuous through it. This is not a hack; it is the discrete counterpart of the well-posedness we demand of the continuous equations.

Part III
Real fluids and phase change

L9Property models and the flash

The ideal-gas closure of Lecture 2 served the sandbox, but steam, refrigerants, and the working fluids of real machines condense, boil, and behave nothing like \(pV=mRT\). The architectural promise made earlier now pays off: only the equation-of-state function changes. We replace the algebraic ideal-gas relations with calls to a property library — CoolProp, REFPROP, the IAPWS steam formulation, or Cantera’s pure-fluid backend — and the rest of the model is untouched.

The operation we need is a flash: from the two intensive coordinates we already carry, \((\rho,u)\), return the full thermodynamic state.

$$ (\rho,\,u)\ \longrightarrow\ \big\{\,p,\ T,\ h,\ s,\ x\,\big\}. $$
(9.1)

Inside the two-phase dome the state is a saturated mixture of liquid and vapour in equilibrium, parameterised by the vapour quality \(x\). Specific volume and internal energy are mass-weighted blends of the saturated-liquid (\(f\)) and saturated-vapour (\(g\)) endpoints,

$$ v = (1-x)v_f + x\,v_g, \qquad u = (1-x)u_f + x\,u_g, \qquad \rho = \frac{1}{v}, $$
(9.2)

so that the quality is recoverable from either conserved coordinate, e.g. \(x = (u-u_f)/(u_g-u_f)\), with \(x<0\) meaning subcooled liquid and \(x>1\) meaning superheated vapour. The library performs this inversion for us, choosing the saturation pressure that makes (9.2) consistent.

Why \((\rho,u)\) is the right coordinate for phase change

During boiling or condensation, pressure and temperature are locked together on the saturation line and barely move while quality sweeps from 0 to 1. A state vector built on \(p\) or \(T\) is therefore nearly singular across the dome — a huge change in the contents produces almost no change in the integrated variable, and the inverse map is ill-conditioned. The conserved coordinates \(\rho\) and \(u\), by contrast, vary monotonically straight through the dome. Integrating \((m,U)\) and flashing to \((p,T)\) is not merely tidy; near phase change it is the difference between a stable simulation and a divergent one.

Two practical points. First, the library returns a phase label alongside the numbers, which we keep for diagnostics (the cylinder may report “liquid”, “two-phase”, “gas”, or “supercritical” at different crank angles). Second, general-purpose flash routines occasionally stall near the saturation boundary; a robust model carries a fallback — because \(u(T,\rho)\) is monotone in \(T\), a simple bisection on temperature at fixed density always converges and resolves quality directly inside the dome. Model A wraps CoolProp’s \((\rho,u)\) flash; Model B’s pure-fluid backend adds exactly such a bisection fallback.

L10Compressible and flashing flow — Model A

The incompressible orifice (6.2) fails for a gas across a large pressure ratio, and fails spectacularly for a saturated or subcooled liquid that flashes — partially vaporises — as it accelerates through the restriction and its pressure falls below the local saturation value. The flashing dramatically increases specific volume, chokes the throat, and limits the achievable mass flux. We need a compressible model that captures both choking and flashing in a single, backflow-capable expression. The homogeneous-equilibrium model (HEM) is the standard tool.

The HEM assumptions

Liquid and vapour move at the same velocity (slip ratio unity) and remain in thermodynamic equilibrium at every point, so at any pressure the mixture properties are the equilibrium saturation values. The two phases are treated as one homogeneous compressible fluid with an equilibrium equation of state.

The companion models use the isentropic-nozzle form of HEM. The donor’s stagnation state \((p_0,h_0)\) fixes an isentrope \(s_0\); for any candidate throat pressure \(p_t\) the equilibrium state on that isentrope gives an enthalpy \(h(p_t,s_0)\) and density \(\rho(p_t,s_0)\), and energy conservation along the nozzle yields the mass flux

$$ G(p_t) = \rho(p_t,s_0)\,\sqrt{2\big(h_0 - h(p_t,s_0)\big)}\,. $$
(10.1)

The delivered flux is the maximum of \(G\) over the admissible throat-pressure range, from the (stabilised) downstream pressure up to the stagnation pressure:

$$ \dot m = C_d A\;\max_{\;p_{\text{down,eff}}\le p_t\le p_0} G(p_t). $$
(10.2)
Choking falls out of a maximisation — no separate branch

As \(p_t\) is lowered from \(p_0\), \(G\) rises, peaks at the critical throat pressure \(p^\*\), then falls. If the actual downstream pressure lies above \(p^\*\), the maximum over the interval sits at the endpoint \(p_{\text{down}}\): the flow is subcritical and the flux is set by the real pressure ratio. If the downstream pressure lies below \(p^\*\), the interval contains the peak, the maximum is \(G(p^\*)\): the flow is choked and the flux is capped, independent of how low the downstream pressure goes. One maximisation — here a few iterations of golden-section search — returns the correct regime automatically. Compare this with the sandbox of Lecture 4, where we hard-coded the choked/subcritical split by hand; the HEM search unifies them.

Fig. 10.1 — Choking as a maximisation. The HEM flux \(G(p_t)\) of (10.1) peaks at the critical pressure \(p^\*\). Taking the maximum over the admissible interval (10.2) returns the choked value when the receiver is below \(p^\*\) and the subcritical value otherwise — no explicit regime test is needed.

single_cylinder_hem.pyexcerpt · HEM throat search
def _G_delivered(self, p_up, p_dn_eff, h0, s0):
    b = np.nextafter(p_up, -np.inf)        # throat strictly below p0   [Eq 8.1]
    a = min(p_dn_eff, b)
    if not (a < b):
        return 0.0
    def G(pt):                             # HEM isentropic mass flux    [Eq 10.1]
        h_t, rho_t = self.fluid.hrho_ps(pt, s0)
        return rho_t * np.sqrt(max(2.0 * (h0 - h_t), 0.0))
    _, G_best = _golden_max(G, a, b, self.n_golden)   # max -> choked OR subcritical [Eq 10.2]
    return G_best

An alternative, equally valid HEM formulation writes a lumped momentum balance across the restriction, \(p_u-p^\*=G^2\big[v^\*-v_u+\tfrac12(K+fL/D)\,\bar v\big]\), and maximises \(G\) the same way; it makes the acceleration, friction, and minor-loss contributions explicit. The isentropic-nozzle form above is what Model A includes.

Part IV
Reacting flows

L11Chemistry inside the control volume — Model B

So far the contents of the cylinder have been a single substance whose only freedom is to change phase. Model B removes that restriction: the control volume now holds a mixture of reacting species — fuel, oxidiser, and combustion products — whose composition evolves in time. This is the step from a steam expander to an internal-combustion engine, and it adds exactly one new idea to the bookkeeping and one new idea to the numerics.

Composition as extra conserved states

A mixture of \(K\) species is described by the species masses \(m_k\), or equivalently the mass fractions \(Y_k=m_k/m\). Each \(m_k\) obeys its own filling/emptying balance, transported by the same port flows that move total mass — each port carries the composition of its donor:

$$ \frac{\mathrm d m_k}{\mathrm d t} \;=\; \sum_{\text{ports}} \dot m_{\text{port}}\; Y_{k,\text{donor}}, \qquad k = 1,\dots,K. $$
(11.1)

The donor rule is the upwinding of Lecture 6 applied to composition: inflow carries the reservoir’s mass fractions, outflow carries the cylinder’s. The state vector grows from \([m,U]\) to \([m,U,m_1,\dots,m_K]\), and the flash of Lecture 9 now takes composition as a third argument: \((\rho,u,Y)\to\{p,T,h,s,\dots\}\). Nothing else in the mass or energy balance changes.

Combustion needs no heat-release source term

One might expect a chemical-energy term \(+\dot Q_{\text{comb}}\) on the right of the energy equation. There is none — and that is the elegant part. The property library uses absolute enthalpies that include each species’ enthalpy of formation. When fuel and oxygen are consumed and products appear, the mixture’s internal energy at fixed \(U\) is simply redistributed: the formation energy released by forming products shows up automatically as a rise in temperature. Energy is conserved by construction. The combustion “heat release” is an emergent consequence of integrating \(U\) and letting composition change underneath it — never a term we add by hand.

Why a single explicit step no longer works

Chemical kinetics introduce stiffness (Lecture 3). Near ignition, radical pools build and collapse on timescales of microseconds while the piston moves on timescales of milliseconds — a separation of three or more orders of magnitude. An explicit RK4 step sized for the piston would be wildly unstable for the chemistry; a step sized for the chemistry would make the simulation impossibly slow. We cannot integrate transport and reaction with one method at one step size.

The standard remedy is operator splitting (Lie splitting). Over each crank-angle step \(\Delta t\) the two physics are advanced in sequence rather than simultaneously:

$$ \underbrace{[m,U,m_k]\ \xrightarrow{\text{RK4},\ \text{frozen }Y}\ [m,U,m_k]^{\!*}}_{\text{transport: valves, wall heat, spark, }p\,\mathrm dV}\quad\longrightarrow\quad \underbrace{[m,U,m_k]^{\!*}\ \xrightarrow{\text{stiff CVODE},\ (m,U,V)\,\text{const}}\ [m,U,m_k]}_{\text{reaction at constant volume \& energy}} $$
(11.2)
The split mirrors the physics

The transport substep advances mass, energy, and species by the port and heat fluxes with composition held frozen — an ordinary non-stiff RK4 step. The reaction substep then holds \((m,U,V)\) fixed and lets the chemistry run to completion over \(\Delta t\) using a dedicated stiff integrator (here Cantera’s CVODE through an IdealGasReactor). Holding \((m,U,V)\) constant during reaction is exactly right: no mass crosses the boundary and no work is done during the chemical relaxation, so internal energy and mass are genuine invariants of that substep and only the species masses change. Each integrator sees only the timescale it is built for.

A practical gate makes this affordable: below a cut-off temperature chem_T_min (here 550 K), hydrocarbon oxidation is frozen on engine timescales, so the expensive CVODE call is skipped entirely. Compression or the spark lifts the charge past the gate; thereafter the kinetics decide on their own whether and when the mixture ignites. Both spark-ignition and spontaneous autoignition (knock, or HCCI with the spark disabled) emerge from the same code with no special-casing.

The spark

The spark is modelled as a deposit of energy — a few hundred joules spread over a couple of crank-degrees — entering the energy equation as a heat term \(\dot Q_{\text{spark}}\). It is deliberately flagged in the source as a single-zone ignition surrogate: a real spark ignites a millimetre-scale kernel that then propagates as a turbulent flame, whereas a lumped model has no spatial extent and must instead raise the whole charge past the kinetic gate. It triggers ignition at the right crank angle, which is the intent here; it is not a flame-propagation model.

The interface is the payoff — one engine, swappable physics

Lecture 9 promised that only the equation-of-state function changes. Model B makes that promise structural: ReactingGas and PureFluid expose the same methods (flash_du, isentrope_hrho, clone), so the identical deriv and HEM-valve code drives either a combusting CH₄/air charge or a condensing steam expander. Swap the backend and the same program becomes a different machine. The single-phase mixture reports quality as NaN; the pure fluid resolves it across the dome — the rest of the model never knows the difference.

def deriv(self, theta_deg, m, U, mk):          # transport only; composition frozen
    V  = self.cyl.V(theta_deg)
    Y  = mk / m if self.fluid.n_species else None
    st = self.fluid.flash_du(m / V, U / m, Y)
    Qw = self.cyl.h_woschni(st["p"], st["T"]) * self.cyl.A_wall(theta_deg) * (self.cyl.T_wall - st["T"])
    Qs = self.spark.Qdot(theta_deg, self.cyl.omega)            # spark heat   [single-zone surrogate]
    dVdt = self.cyl.dVdtheta(theta_deg) * self.cyl.omega
    md_in, h_in, Y_in = self.intake.mass_flow(theta_deg, st, self.supply_state)
    md_ex, h_ex, Y_ex = self.exhaust.mass_flow(theta_deg, st, self.exhaust_state)
    dm  = md_in + md_ex
    dU  = Qw + Qs - st["p"] * dVdt + md_in * h_in + md_ex * h_ex     # NO combustion source term
    dmk = md_in * Y_in + md_ex * Y_ex          # species transported by donor Y   [Eq 11.1]
    return dm, dU, dmk, info

def _chemistry(self, m, U, V, mk, dt):         # stiff reaction at constant (m,U,V)  [Eq 11.2]
    g = self.fluid.gas
    g.UVY = U / m, V / m, mk / m
    if g.T < self.chem_T_min:                  # gate: oxidation frozen below cut-off
        return mk
    net = ct.ReactorNet([ct.IdealGasReactor(g)])
    net.advance(dt)                            # CVODE decides if/when it ignites
    return m * reactor.thermo.Y.copy()

Part V
Coupled systems

L12Networks of control volumes — Model C

A real engine is not one chamber breathing against two infinite reservoirs. It draws through an intake manifold and discharges into an exhaust pipe, and those passages have their own volume, store their own mass and energy, and exhibit their own pressure dynamics — ram, reversion, wave action. Model C takes the decisive structural step of the whole series: it treats the manifolds as control volumes in their own right and couples several of them into a network.

A manifold is just a filling/emptying model with a frozen volume

Every tool from Lectures 4–11 applies to a pipe unchanged — with one simplification. A pipe has no moving boundary, so \(\mathrm dV/\mathrm dt = 0\) and the boundary-work term \(p\,\mathrm dV/\mathrm dt\) vanishes. There is no slider-crank and no Woschni correlation; wall heat is a plain \(\dot Q = UA(T_{\text{wall}}-T)\). What remains is identical: conserved states \([m,U,m_k]\), the same flash, the same HEM ports. A network is therefore not a new kind of model — it is several copies of the one model, integrated together and sharing their ports.

The configuration is the canonical single cylinder with a pipe on each side:

Fig. 12.1 — A three-volume network. Two fixed-volume pipe control volumes flank the moving-volume cylinder. Six HEM restrictions (throttle, two inlet valves, two exhaust valves, tailpipe) couple them and connect the ends to infinite reservoirs. All nine states — three sets of \([m,U,m_k]\) — are advanced in lockstep by one RK4 integrator.

Sign bookkeeping is the one real subtlety

Each valve connects two control volumes, and a single mass flow must appear in both of their balances with opposite sign — what leaves one volume enters the other. The valve routine returns the flow into the cylinder (the convention from Lecture 6); the pipe on the other side therefore sees the negated value. Getting this consistent is what conserves mass across the whole network.

One flow, two ledgers, opposite signs

If the inlet-valve routine reports \(\dot m_{\text{IV}}>0\) meaning gas flowing from the intake pipe into the cylinder, then the cylinder gains \(+\dot m_{\text{IV}}\) and the intake pipe loses it, contributing \(-\dot m_{\text{IV}}\) to the pipe’s \(\mathrm dm/\mathrm dt\). The exhaust side mirrors this: a flow reported as “into the cylinder” through an exhaust valve (reversion) is a loss for the exhaust pipe. A sign error here does not crash the run — it quietly destroys mass, and the only symptom is a slow drift in the global inventory. The conservation check of Lecture 13 exists precisely to catch it.

The throttle and the valves

The valves are the HEM restrictions of Lecture 10 with a crank-angle lift schedule; splitting the intake and exhaust into two valves apiece simply halves each geometric area. The throttle is a butterfly plate whose effective area follows the plate angle \(\theta_t\),

$$ A_{\text{thr}}(\theta_t) \;=\; \frac{\pi D^2}{4}\,\bigl(1 - \cos\theta_t\bigr), $$
(12.1)

and may be held constant or driven by a function of crank angle for transient studies. It is the operator’s load control: a nearly closed plate starves the intake pipe and the manifold pressure falls, throttling the engine exactly as in practice.

Scavenging, and what a single-zone model can and cannot say

When intake and exhaust valves are open together — valve overlap, and the whole working principle of a two-stroke — fresh charge pushes burned gas out while some of it escapes straight through. Because the cylinder is a single well-mixed control volume, Model C represents this as perfect-mixing scavenging: incoming charge mixes instantly with the residual, and the gas leaving through the exhaust valve carries that blend.

Perfect-mixing scavenging — honest about its own limits

The single-zone assumption captures short-circuit loss correctly — some fresh charge does leave unburned, because the exhaust draws the mixed cylinder composition — and it makes the delivery ratio (mass actually delivered per cycle, normalised by displacement) and the gross IMEP rigorous. What it cannot capture is spatial displacement: a real loop- or uniflow-scavenged cylinder pushes burned gas out ahead of the fresh charge with far less mixing, achieving scavenging efficiencies of perhaps 70–85% where perfect mixing predicts only 50–60%. The model therefore under-predicts scavenging efficiency by construction. Knowing that boundary — trusting the delivery ratio, distrusting the absolute trapped-purity — is the difference between using the model and being misled by it.

The companion routine scavenging_metrics() post-processes a completed run into the delivery ratio and gross indicated mean effective pressure (work per cycle divided by displaced volume), the two figures that actually characterise the gas exchange.

Part VI
Practice

L13Verification, validation, and extension

Lecture 1 drew the distinction that now matters most. Verification asks whether we are solving the equations correctly — a question of mathematics and code. Validation asks whether they are the right equations — a question of physics, answered only against experiment. A model can be impeccably verified and still wrong, or physically sound and ruined by a coding slip. Both must be checked, and verification comes first: there is no point comparing a buggy program to data.

Verification 1 — conservation under a closed boundary

Close every valve and the cylinder becomes an isolated mass. With \(\dot m = 0\) at every port, total mass must be constant to round-off for the entire run, regardless of how violently the piston moves. Energy must obey the closed-system statement exactly — the only changes to \(U\) come from wall heat and boundary work,

$$ \frac{\mathrm dm}{\mathrm dt}=0, \qquad \frac{\mathrm dU}{\mathrm dt} = \dot Q_{\text{wall}} - p\,\frac{\mathrm dV}{\mathrm dt}. $$
(13.1)

Set the wall heat to zero as well and the compression–expansion must be reversible: the pressure–volume loop should close on itself with no net area and no entropy generation over a cycle. Any drift in mass, or an open \(p\)–\(V\) loop under adiabatic closed conditions, is a verification failure — almost always a sign-bookkeeping error of the kind Lecture 12 warned about, or a time step too coarse for the integrator.

Verification 2 — the adiabatic rigid-tank filling benchmark

One filling problem has a clean closed-form answer, and every filling/emptying code should reproduce it. Fill an evacuated, rigid, adiabatic tank from a large reservoir held at \((p_s,T_s)\). Rigid means \(\mathrm dV/\mathrm dt=0\) so there is no boundary work; adiabatic means \(\dot Q=0\). The energy balance (Lecture 4) collapses to \(\mathrm dU/\mathrm dt=\dot m\,h_s\), and because the reservoir enthalpy \(h_s\) is constant, integrating from empty gives \(U=m\,h_s\), i.e. the final specific internal energy equals the reservoir specific enthalpy. For an ideal gas, \(u=c_vT\) and \(h_s=c_pT_s\), so

$$ c_v T_{\text{final}} = c_p T_s \quad\Longrightarrow\quad T_{\text{final}} = \gamma\,T_s. $$
(13.2)
Why the filled tank runs hot — and why it is the perfect test

The gas already inside is compressed by the gas flowing in, and the inflow carries flow work \(p_sv_s\) in addition to its internal energy — that is the difference between \(h_s\) and \(u_s\). Both effects deposit energy with no way for it to leave a rigid adiabatic vessel, so the final temperature overshoots the supply temperature by the factor \(\gamma\) (about 1.4 for air — a 40% rise). The result follows from the energy equation alone, independent of the flow law, the valve area, or the time step. Run the ideal-gas sandbox of Lecture 4 to steady fill and it must return \(\gamma T_s\) to several digits. If it does not, the energy bookkeeping — most often a missing or mis-signed flow-work term — is wrong, and no amount of physical realism elsewhere will save the model.

Verification 3 — step refinement

Halve the crank-angle step and the solution should stop changing — and for the RK4 integrator of Lecture 3, the error should fall as roughly the fourth power of the step until property-evaluation round-off sets the floor. If results keep shifting as the step shrinks, the run was never step-converged and any conclusion drawn from it is an artefact of the discretisation, not the physics. Report results only in the converged regime, and quote the step at which convergence was confirmed.

Common failure modes

The companion models encode hard-won defences against four recurring pathologies, each met earlier in these notes:

Four ways a filling/emptying model breaks — and the fix in the code

Division by zero at pressure equality. When a port pressure exactly equals the cylinder pressure, a naive flow law divides by a zero pressure difference and returns NaN, which then poisons every subsequent step. The always-positive-\(\Delta p\) rule of Lecture 8 — the nextafter stabilisation — prevents it structurally.

Flash stalls on the saturation dome. General \((\rho,u)\) inverters can fail to converge right at the phase boundary. The monotone-temperature bisection fallback of Lecture 9 always lands, so the simulation crosses the dome without stalling.

Stiff blow-up from chemistry. Integrating fast kinetics with the explicit transport step detonates the solution numerically. Operator splitting (Lecture 11) quarantines the stiffness in a dedicated implicit substep.

Silent mass loss. A sign error on a shared port flow (Lecture 12) destroys mass with no crash and no warning. Only the closed-boundary conservation check above reveals it — which is why that check is run first and always.

Where to take these models next

Every extension reuses the same architecture — conserved states in, flash, ports, integrate — and adds states or elements without disturbing what exists. A natural progression:

  • Dynamic piston. Replace the prescribed slider-crank with a free piston by promoting position and velocity to states \([m,U,x,\dot x]\) and adding Newton’s second law with pressure, spring, friction, and load forces. The volume is then solved with the thermodynamics rather than imposed — the route to free-piston engines, pneumatic actuators, and bounce chambers.
  • Pipes with inertia and friction. Upgrade the fixed-volume manifolds from lumped plenums toward lumped-momentum or one-dimensional gas dynamics, capturing the ram and wave-tuning effects a single well-mixed volume cannot represent.
  • More volumes, more cylinders. The network of Lecture 12 extends directly to multi-cylinder engines sharing a plenum, to turbocharger volumes, or to any plant of vessels and restrictions — each volume one more copy of the same model.
  • Richer thermodynamics and chemistry. Swap the property backend (Lecture 9), enlarge the reaction mechanism, add pollutant or soot kinetics, or model a separated two-phase vessel by drawing saturated vapour or saturated liquid from the appropriate port rather than the mixed contents.

The thread running through all thirteen lectures is a single discipline: integrate the quantities nature conserves, evaluate everything else from them, and let the difficult behaviour — choking, flashing, ignition, reversion, scavenging — emerge from honest equations rather than be patched in by hand. Master that, and the three companion programs are not three different models but one idea, deepened.

A.4   Exercises

These run from pure verification through physical extension, roughly in order of difficulty.

  1. Closed-boundary conservation. Take the ideal-gas sandbox of Lecture 4, shut both valves, and integrate for several piston cycles with the wall heat first off, then on. Confirm mass is constant to round-off in both cases, and that the adiabatic \(p\)–\(V\) loop closes with zero net area. Plot the mass-conservation residual against time.
  2. The \(\gamma T_s\) benchmark. Fill a rigid, adiabatic tank from a reservoir and verify the steady result \(T_{\text{final}}=\gamma T_s\) of (13.2) to three significant figures. Then add wall heat and explain — physically, before re-running — whether the final temperature rises above or falls below \(\gamma T_s\).
  3. Order of accuracy. Run Model A at a sequence of halving crank-angle steps and plot the change in a cycle-integrated quantity (say, work per cycle) against step size on log axes. Confirm the RK4 slope until the property round-off floor appears, and identify the step at which the result is converged.
  4. Choking by inspection. For a fixed donor state in Model A, evaluate \(G(p_t)\) of (10.1) across the throat-pressure interval and plot it. Locate the peak \(p^\*\), then drive the receiver pressure above and below it and confirm the delivered flux switches between the endpoint and the capped peak exactly as (10.2) predicts.
  5. Spark timing. In Model B’s SI build, sweep the spark crank angle and record peak pressure and gross work per cycle. Find the timing that maximises work, and relate the loss at over-advanced and over-retarded timing to where combustion falls relative to top dead centre.
  6. Autoignition without a spark. Disable the spark and raise the compression ratio (or supply temperature) until the charge autoignites from compression alone. Identify the onset condition and discuss it as the boundary between HCCI operation and knock.
  7. Scavenging trade-off. In Model C’s two-stroke build, vary the valve-overlap window and plot delivery ratio against the short-circuited fresh-charge fraction. Explain why the perfect-mixing assumption makes the delivery ratio trustworthy but the absolute trapped purity pessimistic.
  8. Free-piston extension. Promote piston position and velocity to states and add Newton’s second law (pressure, spring, friction, load), as sketched in Lecture 13. Reproduce the prescribed-motion result in the stiff-spring limit, then explore a genuinely free bounce.
